Adobo

A Filipino style of cooking

Filipino pork adobo is a popular and beloved dish in Philippine cuisine. It's a savory and tangy stew typically made with pork belly or shoulder, although other cuts of pork can also be used.

The hallmark of Filipino Adobo is its savory, tangy, and slightly sweet taste profile. It's achieved through the braising or stewing process, where meat is simmered in a mixture of vinegar, soy sauce, garlic, bay leaves, and black peppercorns.

Ingredients in Cooking a Delicious Adobo:

  • Pork: Traditionally, pork belly or pork shoulder is used, but other cuts such as pork loin can also be used.
  • Soy sauce: This provides the salty base for the marinade and sauce.
  • Vinegar: Usually white vinegar or cane vinegar is used, imparting a tangy flavor to the dish.
  • Garlic: Crushed or minced garlic cloves are essential for adding aromatic flavor.
  • Bay leaves: These dried aromatic leaves add depth and fragrance to the dish.
  • Whole black peppercorns: They provide a subtle heat and flavor to the marinade.
  • Water: Used to dilute the marinade and simmer the pork until tender.
  • Optional ingredients: Some variations may include additional ingredients such as onions, sugar, or coconut milk to enhance the flavor and richness of the dish.

Steps in Cooking a Delicious Adobo:

  1. Marinate the pork: In a large bowl or container, combine the pork chunks, soy sauce, vinegar, minced garlic, bay leaves, and whole black peppercorns. Mix well to ensure the pork is evenly coated with the marinade. Cover the bowl or container and refrigerate for at least 1 hour, preferably overnight, to allow the flavors to meld.
  2. Cook the pork: Transfer the marinated pork and marinade to a large skillet or pot. Add water to the pot until the pork is almost covered. If you're using additional ingredients like sliced onions, add them to the pot now. Bring the mixture to a boil over medium-high heat.
  3. Simmer: Once the mixture is boiling, reduce the heat to low and let it simmer, uncovered, for about 45 minutes to 1 hour, or until the pork is tender and the sauce has reduced and thickened. Stir occasionally to prevent sticking and to ensure even cooking.
  4. Adjust seasoning: Taste the adobo and adjust the seasoning if necessary. If you prefer a sweeter adobo, you can add sugar to taste at this point. If you're using coconut milk, you can add it now for a richer flavor.
  5. Serve: Once the pork is tender and the sauce has thickened to your liking, remove the bay leaves and discard them. Transfer the pork adobo to a serving dish and serve hot with steamed rice.
